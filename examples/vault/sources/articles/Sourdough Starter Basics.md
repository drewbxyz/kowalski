# Sourdough Starter Basics

A sourdough starter is a live culture of wild yeast and lactic acid bacteria kept
alive in a simple mixture of flour and water. Given regular feedings, the culture
stabilizes into a reliable leavening agent that can raise bread without any
commercial yeast.

## Making a starter

Mix equal weights of whole-grain flour and room-temperature water in a clean jar.
Cover loosely and leave it at room temperature. Once a day, discard about half of
the mixture and feed the remainder with fresh flour and water at the same equal
weights. Bubbles usually appear within two or three days; a dependable rise-and-fall
rhythm takes closer to a week or two.

## Feeding and maintenance

- Feed once a day at room temperature, or store the starter in the refrigerator
  and feed it about once a week.
- A starter is ready to use when it roughly doubles in volume within four to
  eight hours of a feeding.
- The discard step keeps acidity in check and gives the fresh flour a fighting
  chance; skipping it produces a sluggish, overly sour culture.

## Troubleshooting

A layer of gray liquid on top ("hooch") just means the starter is hungry — pour it
off or stir it in, then feed. Pink or orange streaks, however, indicate spoilage:
discard the culture and start over. A slow starter usually needs warmth more than
it needs a new recipe; somewhere around 24-27 C (75-80 F) is the comfortable range
for wild yeast activity.
